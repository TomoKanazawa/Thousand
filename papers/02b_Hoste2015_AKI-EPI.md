## Abstract (verbatim, from publisher page)

> Current reports on acute kidney injury (AKI) in the intensive care unit (ICU) show wide variation in occurrence rate and are limited by study biases such as use of incomplete AKI definition, selected cohorts, or retrospective design. We sought to prospectively investigate the occurrence and outcomes of AKI in ICU patients.
>
> The Acute Kidney Injury–Epidemiologic Prospective Investigation (AKI-EPI) study was an international cross-sectional study performed in 97 centers, recruiting patients during a 10-day period. AKI was measured by Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) criteria, and outcomes were assessed at hospital discharge.
>
> A total of 1032 ICU patients out of 1802 [57.3 %; 95 % confidence interval (CI) 55.0–59.6] had AKI. Increasing AKI severity was associated with hospital mortality when adjusted for other variables; odds ratio of stage 1 = 1.679 (95 % CI 0.890–3.169; p = 0.109), stage 2 = 2.945 (95 % CI 1.382–6.276; p = 0.005), and stage 3 = 6.884 (95 % CI 3.876–12.228; p <0.001).
>
> AKI occurs in more than half of ICU patients. Increasing severity of AKI is associated with increased mortality.

## Key numbers

- **AKI incidence in ICU:** 1,032 / 1,802 = **57.3%** (95% CI 55.0–59.6) using KDIGO criteria
- **Study scope:** 97 centers, international, prospective, 10-day recruitment window
- **Hospital-mortality adjusted OR by stage:**
  - Stage 1: 1.679 (95% CI 0.890–3.169) — not statistically significant (p=0.109)
  - Stage 2: 2.945 (95% CI 1.382–6.276) — p=0.005
  - Stage 3: 6.884 (95% CI 3.876–12.228) — p<0.001
- **Implication:** AKI is the single most common organ dysfunction in ICU patients

## Why this matters for Andy

This is one of two foundational citations (with Susantitaphong 2013 #02a / Cammarata 2024 #02c) needed to defend the **AKI inpatient prevalence rate** in SAM cell 1. AKI-EPI gives the **ICU-specific** rate (57.3%) which is critical because:

1. ICU admissions are a high-stakes subset where DRG uplift per captured AKI is largest (often shifts to MCC tier).
2. AHRQ NIS shows ~5.7M ICU admissions/year in the US — applied with 57.3% AKI incidence → ~3.3M ICU AKI cases alone, before adding ward AKI from Susantitaphong's 22% ward rate.

Combined with the Cammarata 68% undercoding rate, this transforms the AKI inpatient DRG-uplift cell from "reasoned estimate" to "fully cited" and unlocks the ~$10B AKI ceiling in the SAM ladder.

## Caveats

- **ICU-only** — does not address ward / general medical AKI rates. Use Susantitaphong 2013 meta-analysis (22% pooled hospitalized) for the ward complement.
- **Multinational (not US-specific)** — most centers were European/Asian; AKI rates may differ in US ICU populations due to case mix, but the consensus is rates are similar or slightly higher in US ICUs.
- **2014 data** — predates the wider adoption of KDIGO criteria in clinical workflow. If anything, current rates may be detected at higher frequency.
