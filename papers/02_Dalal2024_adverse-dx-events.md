## Abstract
Background: Standard surveillance methods fail to capture the full scope of harmful diagnostic mistakes in hospital settings. Researchers conducted a single-centre retrospective examination of hospitalized general medicine patients using stratified sampling based on ICU transfer, 90-day mortality, complex clinical presentations, and lower-risk cases. Two independent reviewers assessed diagnostic error likelihood using the Safer Dx instrument, characterized harm and preventability, and identified process failures. Expert panels reviewed uncertain cases.

Results: Among 9,147 eligible patients, 675 were sampled (100% of ICU transfers, 38.5% of deaths within 90 days, 7% of complex cases, 2.4% of lower-risk cases). Population estimates indicated harmful diagnostic errors in 7.2% (95% CI 4.66–9.80), preventable errors in 6.1% (95% CI 3.79–8.50), and severely harmful errors in 1.1% (95% CI 0.55–1.68). Delays characterized 61.9% of harmful errors. Process failures in assessment, testing, subspecialty consultation, patient experience, and history were significantly associated with harmful diagnostic errors.

Conclusions: Roughly 1 in 14 general medicine inpatients experienced preventable harmful diagnostic errors, highlighting the necessity for improved surveillance approaches.

## Key numbers
- 7.2% of inpatients had harmful diagnostic errors (95% CI 4.66–9.80)
- 6.1% preventable; 1.1% severely harmful
- 61.9% of harmful errors involved delays
- ~1 in 14 general medicine inpatients affected
