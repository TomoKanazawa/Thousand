# 10 — Autopsy discrepancy studies (Shojania meta-analysis + ICU meta)

> **Stub (no PDF).** Compiled from abstracts / PSNet / PubMed.

## Primary: Shojania 2003 (the landmark autopsy meta-analysis)
- **Citation:** Shojania KG, Burton EC, McDonald KM, Goldman L. *Changes in rates of autopsy-detected diagnostic errors over time: a systematic review.* JAMA. 2003;289(21):2849–2856. PMID 12783916.
- **Design / N:** Systematic review of **53 autopsy series**, literature 1966–2002.
- **Key numbers:**
  - Median **major error rate 23.5%** (range 4.1–49.8%).
  - Median **Class I error rate 9.0%** (errors that, if known, likely would have changed management/survival).
  - Declining over time: **−19.4%/decade (major), −33.4%/decade (Class I)**.
  - Contemporary estimates: major **8.4–24.4%**, Class I **4.1–6.7%**.

## Secondary: ICU autopsy meta-analysis 2024
- **Citation:** *Clinical vs. autopsy diagnostic discrepancies in the intensive care unit: a systematic review and meta-analysis of autopsy series.* Intensive Care Med. 2024. PMID 39287650.
- Modern ICU-specific update of the clinical-vs-autopsy discrepancy rate (Class I missed diagnoses persist).

## Relevance as a data source (PARTIAL — flagged in list)
- **Gold-standard truth** (autopsy) for what was *actually* wrong → the strongest possible "ground truth" for missed diagnosis.
- **But:** no **antemortem note-level data** released — it's aggregate discrepancy rates, not a patient-level chart corpus. So it supports the *prevalence/why-it-matters* argument, **not** a usable training/validation dataset.
